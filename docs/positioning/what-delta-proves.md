# What DELTA Proves — and What It Does Not Prove

## DELTA Proves

DELTA can prove cryptographic relationships between:

- a change record and its canonical hash;
- a record and a digital signature;
- a record and its evidence hashes;
- a record and a replay result;
- a record and a signed intent;
- a record and encrypted auditor evidence;
- a record and a publication proof;
- a record and a trust ledger entry;
- a record and a wallet/address-control proof.

The strongest DELTA claim is:

```text
This specific proof object is cryptographically bound to this specific change record hash.
```

## DELTA Does Not Prove

DELTA does not automatically prove:

- legal truth;
- real-world truth;
- regulatory compliance;
- business approval;
- legal identity;
- legal ownership of assets;
- wallet balance;
- authority of a signer;
- honesty of the machine that generated a record;
- that private evidence is true;
- that external APIs returned truthful data.

## Why This Boundary Matters

A protocol that overclaims becomes unsafe.

DELTA is intentionally precise. It proves what cryptography can prove and leaves legal, organizational, and real-world claims to external governance layers.

## Positioning

A short positioning statement:

```text
The internet can prove ownership.
DELTA proves change.
```

Expanded:

```text
DELTA turns a change into a cryptographic object that can be hashed, signed, replayed, audited, published, chained, and bound to wallet/address-control proofs.
```
